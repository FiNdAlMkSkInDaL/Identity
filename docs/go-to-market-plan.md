# Go-To-Market Plan: Identity

This document turns Identity's product thesis into an initial commercial plan.

The monetization objective is to prove that local-first context ownership can drive measurable task quality and trust for high-value operators before Identity is sold as protocol infrastructure.

## 1. GTM Thesis

Identity should enter the market first as a premium local operating memory for users whose work quality depends on private context, relationship recall, and delegated execution.

The opening product is not a general consumer assistant and not a developer protocol sale. It is a high-trust local productivity system for users with expensive mistakes and expensive context loss.

Commercial sequence:

1. Win individual operators with a premium desktop product.
2. Expand into teams that need shared policy and review controls.
3. Sell enterprise deployment, security, and compliance features.
4. Only after product pull is proven, expose partner SDK and protocol monetization.

## 2. Ideal Customer Profile

### Primary ICP

The first ICP is high-agency knowledge workers who manage dense relationship graphs, sensitive information, and repeated outbound work.

Best-fit users:

- Venture-backed founders.
- Investors and deal teams.
- Executive recruiters and talent partners.
- Business development and partnerships leads.
- Chiefs of staff and executive operators.

Common traits:

- They run high-value workflows across email, docs, CRM-like systems, browser research, and notes.
- Their output quality improves materially when personal context is available.
- They are already using AI tools but do not trust them with full memory persistence.
- They are willing to pay for judgment quality, speed, and privacy rather than token volume.

### Sharp Exclusion Criteria

Do not target these segments first:

- Broad consumer productivity.
- Students and low-intent hobby users.
- Developers looking only for an API or open protocol library.
- Large enterprises before the admin, policy, and deployment controls exist.

### Beachhead Buyer Narrative

The first buyer should be able to say:

"I need an assistant that remembers my context across work, improves every week, and does not hand my operating history to someone else's cloud."

## 3. Positioning

### Category

Identity is a local-first operating memory for agentic work.

### Positioning Statement

For high-value operators who use AI but cannot trust cloud memory products with durable context, Identity is a local-first context and delegation layer that turns personal work history into scoped, ephemeral task context without surrendering the full memory graph to a third party.

### Why It Wins

- Better outputs because the system can retrieve real personal context.
- Better trust because the master state stays local.
- Better long-term value because memory compounds instead of resetting every session.
- Better strategic position because the product can later become the context rail for multiple agent endpoints.

## 4. Launch Wedge

### Wedge Definition

Launch around one painful, high-frequency problem: high-stakes outbound communication that depends on memory quality.

Initial wedge:

- Relationship-aware drafting for founder, investor, and business development outreach.

This wedge is stronger than a generic assistant because it directly uses Identity's differentiator:

- prior conversations,
- notes,
- writing tone,
- target-specific context,
- task-scoped retrieval,
- local persistence.

### Why This Wedge First

- Users already spend money and attention here.
- Quality is easy to judge quickly.
- The privacy story is intuitive.
- Success creates obvious adjacent workflows in research and follow-up.
- It does not require full autonomous action to prove value.

### Core Promise

"Draft the right message with the right context, in my voice, without leaking my private operating memory."

## 5. First Three Paid Workflows

The first paid workflows should be narrow, repeated, and visibly better because of Identity's memory model.

### Workflow 1: Relationship-Aware Outreach Drafting

User outcome:

- Draft investor, founder, candidate, or partner outreach using prior interactions, notes, shared context, and tone memory.

Why users pay:

- Bad outbound costs meetings.
- Generic AI drafts are easy to spot.
- Identity can use local relationship memory without exposing the full graph.

Ideal proof metric:

- User accepts or lightly edits the first draft in under 5 minutes.

### Workflow 2: Meeting and Research Brief Generation

User outcome:

- Generate a pre-call brief combining local notes, prior conversations, relevant documents, recent activity, and target background.

Why users pay:

- Preparation quality directly affects meeting outcomes.
- Users already collect this context manually across tabs and notes.
- Identity can package just enough context into one reusable brief.

Ideal proof metric:

- User can prepare for a meeting in 10 minutes instead of 30 to 60 minutes.

### Workflow 3: Follow-Up and CRM Memory Capture

User outcome:

- Turn calls, notes, and sent messages into structured follow-ups and local memory updates, with suggested next actions.

Why users pay:

- Work is lost after the meeting unless context is captured cleanly.
- CRM hygiene is universally painful.
- This workflow begins proving Identity's feedback-loop advantage.

Ideal proof metric:

- User leaves each meeting with a ready-to-send follow-up and an updated local record.

## 6. Offer Design

### Product Packaging

The initial commercial offer should be a desktop subscription with a clear promise around private context retrieval and task quality.

Plan structure:

- Pro: for individual operators.
- Team: for small firms that need shared governance and workspace controls.
- Enterprise: for regulated or security-sensitive deployments.

### Suggested Pricing

#### Pro

- $49 per month annual or $79 month-to-month.
- Includes local memory, context retrieval, prompt packaging, and the first paid workflows.

#### Team

- $149 per seat per month with a team minimum.
- Adds workspace policy, shared operating templates, review flows, and admin controls once available.

#### Enterprise

- Custom pricing with annual minimum commitment.
- Adds managed deployment, policy enforcement, security review, auditability, and later secure-execution options.

### Pricing Logic

- Price against outcome value, not model cost.
- Keep the Pro plan expensive enough to qualify serious users.
- Use annual discounts to improve retention and cash collection.
- Avoid a cheap freemium tier that attracts curiosity without workflow commitment.

## 8. Distribution Strategy

### Initial Channels

- Founder and investor networks.
- Design-partner outreach to small funds, founder offices, and recruiter boutiques.
- Product essays demonstrating why cloud memory is the wrong abstraction.
- Live workflow demos showing before-and-after output quality.

### Sales Motion

Start with founder-led sales.

Target motion:

1. Recruit 10 to 15 design partners in the primary ICP.
2. Implement the three paid workflows tightly around those users.
3. Convert design partners into paid annual Pro or Team plans.
4. Use concrete output improvements as the core case study asset.

### Proof Assets

- Before-and-after draft quality examples.
- Time saved in meeting prep.
- Follow-up completion rate.
- Qualitative trust feedback about local memory versus cloud assistants.

## 9. 90-Day GTM Execution Plan

### Days 1-30

- Finalize the ICP and exclusion criteria.
- Recruit 10 design partners from founder, investor, and recruiter circles.
- Build the workflow demo for outreach drafting first.
- Write the initial pricing page and waitlist flow.

Success checkpoint:

- At least 5 design partners actively use the outreach workflow weekly.

### Days 31-60

- Add meeting brief generation.
- Instrument workflow completion and edit-rate metrics.
- Run weekly design-partner interviews.
- Start charging the earliest users who reach repeated value.

Success checkpoint:

- At least 3 users agree to pay for continued access.

### Days 61-90

- Add follow-up plus memory-capture workflow.
- Publish two or three strong customer stories.
- Introduce annual Pro pricing.
- Begin small-team sales where more than one operator shares a workflow.

Success checkpoint:

- At least 10 paying users or 2 paying teams.

## 10. Metrics That Matter

Track these metrics before broadening the market:

- Weekly active users in the target ICP.
- Percentage of tasks completed with low edit distance.
- Time-to-useful-output for each workflow.
- Retention after 4 weeks.
- Conversion from design partner to paid.
- Number of users storing repeated high-trust context locally.

Do not over-weight vanity metrics such as waitlist size, raw signups, or generic chat volume.

## 11. Expansion Path

After the first workflows and paid retention are proven, expand in this order:

1. Small-team coordination and review workflows.
2. Enterprise security, deployment, and policy features.
3. Partner SDK for scoped context access.
4. Protocol and ecosystem monetization.

Protocol monetization should be treated as a scale-stage layer, not the opening revenue engine.
